A kitchen renovation is one of the highest-stakes projects you can take on in your own home. The budget is usually the largest single line item outside the property purchase itself. The timeline stretches across months. And unlike a bathroom reno — where you can relocate to the guest bath for a week — a kitchen is the engine of your house. When it stops, everything slows down.

I've watched three kitchen renovations start to finish — two of my own, one for a friend. The timeline I'm about to give you is a real kitchen in a 1970s Australian house, full tear-out, new cabinetry, new countertops, new flooring, new appliances, moved plumbing, and new electrical. Not a cosmetic refresh. Not a cabinet swap. The full deal.

The timeline below is week-by-week, from the first demo day to the punch list walkthrough. But there's a hidden timeline before that: design and permits. Most renovators underestimate it by 3–4 weeks.

## The hidden timeline: design and permits (weeks –4 to 0)

Before the first hammer swings, you need:

**Week –4 to –3: Design.** Finalise your kitchen layout, appliance schedule, and finish selections. This means choosing cabinet style, countertop material, backsplash tile, paint colours, fixture hardware, and lighting plan. Every decision that involves "let me think about it" on day 60 of demolition is a budget and schedule risk.

**Week –3 to –2: Permits.** Submit plans to your local building authority. This includes electrical layouts, plumbing changes, structural changes (moving walls, enlarging openings), and sometimes energy compliance documentation. Permit review timelines vary wildly by council or municipality — two weeks to eight weeks, sometimes longer. **Do not start demolition until your permit is approved.** Building officials will shut down your project on the spot if they show up during demo and there's no permit on site.

**Week –2 to –1: Order long-lead items.** Cabinets, countertops, and specialty appliances (induction cooktops, built-in ovens, large refrigerators) have lead times ranging from one week to twelve weeks. Order them during the permit phase, not after demo starts. This is where the most common timeline disasters happen — contractors tearing out a perfectly good kitchen because someone forgot that custom cabinets take three months to arrive.

| Item | Typical lead time | When to order |
|---|---|---|
| Stock cabinets | 1–2 weeks | After design is finalised |
| Semi-custom cabinets | 6–8 weeks | After permit is in hand |
| Custom cabinets | 10–14 weeks | As soon as design is locked |
| Countertops | 2–4 weeks (after template) | After cabinets are measured |
| Specialty appliances | 2–6 weeks | After design is finalised |
| Standard appliances | 0–2 weeks | Any time |

**What's happening now:** You're still living in your house, still using your kitchen. This is the last peaceful phase. It's also where the real decisions happen — the ones that determine whether your renovation finishes on time or gets stretched by mid-project change orders.

## Week 1: Demo and tear-out

The contractors arrive at 7am. The first thing they do is disconnect the gas line, water lines, and electrical feeds. Then the demolition starts — old cabinets come down, countertops are chipped off, flooring is ripped up, and anything that's getting replaced goes into a skip or dumpster.

**What to expect:** Loud, dusty, messy. The room becomes a construction zone in about four hours. Everything you own in that kitchen (dishes, food, small appliances) needs to have been moved days before.

**What you see:** A shell. Walls, floor, and ceiling exposed. It looks like a disaster. This is normal.

**What's behind the walls:** Demo is also an investigation phase. Once the old cabinets come down, you might find water damage, outdated wiring, or structural issues that need addressing before you can close the walls again. If you find anything, the project pauses for an engineering assessment and possibly a permit amendment. That's the timeline risk I mentioned.

**Duration:** 1–3 days for a standard kitchen. Larger kitchens or those with structural changes take 3–5 days.

## Week 2: Rough-in (electrical, plumbing, HVAC)

This is the phase where the invisible infrastructure gets replaced. New wiring goes up, new water and drain lines are run, gas lines are re-piped, and any HVAC work (ductwork for range hoods, new vents) is completed.

**What's happening:**
- Electricians install new circuits for appliances, dedicated lines for the fridge, lighting circuits, and outlets for the countertop (now you'll want at least four, not two).
- Plumbers run new water supply and drain lines to the new cabinet layout. The sink, dishwasher, and fridge ice maker all get new connections.
- HVAC work: range hood ducting (especially important for induction or high-BTU gas cooktops), any ventilation changes.

**Inspections:** At the end of rough-in, the building inspector visits. They check that all electrical, plumbing, and structural work meets code. **This is a critical path item.** If the inspector flags something — and they will, every time, on at least one thing — your timeline pauses until the issue is fixed. The fix usually takes 1–2 days. But if there's a backlog of inspections, you might wait a week for the re-inspection. Build that buffer in.

**Duration:** 3–5 days of active work, plus 1–3 days for the inspection (depending on council backlog).

## Week 3: Walls close up, floor goes down

Once rough-in passes inspection, the walls get closed up. Drywall is hung, taped, mudded, and sanded. In a kitchen, you'll usually want moisture-resistant drywall (green board) in the splash zone behind the sink and stove.

The subfloor is prepped and finished — new plywood if the old one was damaged during demo, or a levelling compound if the existing floor is uneven.

**What you see:** The room starts looking like a room again. But it's still not a kitchen. The walls are white and smooth, the floor is bare subfloor, and the only thing in the room is drywall dust.

**Important note:** Don't paint yet. Paint comes after the cabinets are installed, so you can cut a clean line at the backsplash zone and touch up any scuffs from cabinet installers.

**Duration:** 4–6 days for drywall hang + finish. The sanding dust will settle for another week, but you can walk in after 3–4 days.

## Week 4: Cabinets arrive and get installed

This is the moment you've been waiting for since the design phase. The cabinets come in and get stacked against the walls. A cabinet installer or carpenter levels, secures, and sequences them — base cabinets first, then wall cabinets, then tall units.

**What happens:**
- Base cabinets are installed and levelled. Every cabinet needs to be perfectly level because countertops sit on top of them and a crooked cabinet means a crooked counter.
- Wall cabinets are hung. Their height is determined by the countertop and the range hood (if there is one). Standard height is 54cm from the countertop, but this varies.
- Tall cabinets (pantry, appliance garage, oven tower) go in last.

**The critical path:** If your cabinets haven't arrived by week 4, the entire project is on hold. The contractors can't move forward without cabinets because the countertops can't be templated and installed until the cabinet boxes are in place. This is the single most common timeline disaster.

**Duration:** 2–4 days for cabinet installation, depending on size and complexity.

**What's happening in parallel:** If your cabinets have arrived, the countertemplate order can be placed. This means a technician comes in, measures the exact layout of your cabinets, and produces a digital template that gets sent to the slab fabricator. Countertop templating happens after cabinets are in. The actual countertop install comes later.

## Week 5: Countertops and backsplash start

With cabinets in place and templated, the countertop slab arrives and gets installed. This is one of the most satisfying days of any renovation — suddenly, the kitchen looks real. Quartz, granite, or whatever material you chose is set into place, seams are minimised, and the edges are finished.

The backsplash work starts — tiling begins from the countertop up. Depending on your backsplash design, this can take anywhere from half a day (simple subway tile) to 3–4 days (complex patterns, mosaic, or hand-painted tiles).

**What's happening:**
- Countertop installers cut the slab on-site (especially for stone), seal it, and set it on the cabinets.
- Tile installers work on the backsplash. They also often install the kitchen floor at the same time if the flooring is tile — but many people lay the flooring *before* the cabinets, so this depends on your choice.

**Duration:** 1 day for countertop install (stone needs 2–3 days for fabrication + delivery). Backsplash: 1–4 days depending on complexity.

## Week 6: Flooring, paint, and appliance delivery

By this point, several sub-trades should be converging:

**Flooring:** If the floor is vinyl, laminate, or engineered wood, it's laid now. If it's tile, it may have started during the backsplash phase. Hardwood is usually laid before the cabinets go in (under-cabinet height), which is why the timeline above has it here rather than earlier.

**Paint:** Walls are painted now. The ceiling was painted during the drywall phase, but the walls get their finish coat after the cabinets are installed so you can cut a clean line where the cabinets meet the wall.

**Appliances:** If they arrived, they're staged. If they didn't, they're not. The appliances that matter most right now are the range hood (needs ducting connected), the dishwasher (needs plumbing), and the fridge (needs a dedicated electrical circuit).

**Duration:** 2–4 days for flooring, 1–2 days for paint. Appliances arrive and are staged — not installed yet, because the final plumbing and electrical connections happen next.

## Week 7: Rough-in review, final plumbing and electrical

This week is about finishing the mechanical work:

- The plumber connects the sink, dishwasher, and fridge water lines.
- The electrician installs outlets, switch plates, under-cabinet lighting, range hood wiring, and connects the appliance circuits.
- The range hood is ducted and tested.
- The cooktop and oven are connected (gas or electric).

**What's happening:** This is the transition from "construction zone" to "kitchen." The lights come on. The water runs. The outlets work. But the counters are still bare in places where appliances will sit, and the cabinets are still missing handles and drawer slides.

**Duration:** 2–3 days.

## Week 8: Appliances installed, handles and hardware on

The final install phase:

- Countertop cutouts are finished for cooktop and sink.
- The sink is grouted in and caulked.
- The cooktop and oven are set in.
- The dishwasher is plumbed and electrically connected.
- The fridge is placed (not connected, just sitting there).
- Cabinet handles, knobs, and drawer slides are attached.
- The range hood is mounted and connected.

**What you see:** A fully functional kitchen. It works. You can plug in a kettle, run the faucet, and open a cabinet door. But it's not finished — there are still unpainted touch-ups, still grout to clean, still a few things to adjust.

**Duration:** 1–2 days.

## Week 9: Touch-ups and cleaning

The final two weeks of any renovation always expand. This is where the punch list happens:

- Paint touch-ups where cabinets were scuffed during install.
- Caulk lines re-done in places where the first attempt wasn't clean.
- Grout haze removed from countertops and tile.
- Cabinet doors adjusted so they close properly and don't rub.
- Sink and faucet caulked and tested for leaks.
- The floor is deep-cleaned.

**What's happening:** The contractors are coming back for the things they missed or that needed correction. This is normal. Every project has a punch list. The size of your punch list depends on how well the initial trades did their work and how picky you are — both of which vary widely.

**Duration:** 3–5 days.

## Week 10: Final walkthrough and punch list closure

The contractor brings you through the kitchen room by room. Every drawer opens. Every cabinet closes. Every outlet works. Every faucet runs. Every appliance is tested. Anything that doesn't make the list gets a note.

**What happens:**
- You walk through with the contractor, testing everything.
- Each issue gets logged on a punch list (again, the printable [renovation checklist](/blog/renovation-checklist-printable/) is the right format).
- The contractor commits to fixing each item by a specific date.

This is the moment the renovation transitions from construction to completion. Everything after this is remedial.

**Duration:** 1 day for the walkthrough, 2–5 days for punch list items to be fixed.

## What the timeline doesn't tell you

There are two things the week-by-week breakdown above never captures, and they matter as much as the schedule itself.

**The decision fatigue.** By week four, you've made 200 decisions about this kitchen. Cabinet handle style, which outlet to put where, whether the island needs an overhang, what colour grout goes with the tile. Each one seems small. Cumulatively, they exhaust you. The timeline above assumes you've already made all these decisions before week one. That's the right way to plan — but the fatigue is real even if you planned well.

**The budget creep.** This timeline gives you the duration, but the financial timeline is its own animal. Each week that passes with a partially demolished kitchen is money you're spending without getting anything back. The contractors are on site, the permits are paid, the demolition happened. And the cabinet order is paid but the cabinets aren't installed. The budget tracking you set up [before you started](/blog/how-to-budget-a-home-renovation/) is what keeps this from spiralling — but you need to be logging every expense *as it happens*, which is exactly the problem [Home Stories](https://apps.apple.com/dk/app/home-stories-renovation-app/id6754754960) is designed for.

## Why this timeline is realistic (not optimistic)

The timelines you see in magazine features and design blogs assume a dream scenario: cabinets arrive on time, inspections pass first try, no surprises behind the walls, and every sub-trade shows up when they're supposed to. That happens on curated projects with dedicated project managers and unlimited budgets.

This timeline is built for a real renovation with a real contractor, a real council, and real supply chains. It includes:

- A week built into inspections for re-inspection if needed.
- Cabinet lead time factored into the pre-demo phase.
- A full week of punch list work (not the two days magazines show).
- The reality that countertop fabrication has a separate timeline from installation.
- The fact that multiple trades need to coordinate on the same days (plumber and electrician, for example, need to be available in week 7).

If you want to compress this timeline, the only reliable lever is ordering everything early — cabinets, countertops, appliances, and fixtures. The second is choosing stock or semi-custom rather than custom, which saves 4–8 weeks on the cabinet lead time.

But even with both of those, 8 weeks is the floor for a full kitchen reno. Anything faster usually means shortcuts — and shortcuts in a kitchen renovation show up in the next five years as leaky sinks, warped cabinets, and outlets that trip the breaker.
