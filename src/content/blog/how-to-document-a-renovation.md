There's a particular kind of regret that shows up long after a renovation finishes. It isn't about the work — the work is usually fine. It's about the *record* of the work: the photo they didn't take of the open wall, the model number they couldn't recall when the boiler needed replacing, the quote that lives only in the fading memory of two people who haven't spoken since the invoice landed.

Documentation is not glamorous. Nobody posts a photo of their wall-stud archive on Instagram. But it is the thing that separates a renovation that runs smoothly from one that devolves into "I thought you said…" and "I'm pretty sure we agreed…"

This isn't about being obsessive. It's about recognising that a house has a memory, and you're the only one who's going to keep it.

## What counts as documentation

Before we get into the *how*, let's agree on what we're talking about. A renovation document is any dated record that captures what something looked like, how much it cost, or what was agreed. It includes:

- Photographs — before shots, during shots, after shots
- Receipts and invoices, ideally photographed at the till or scanned
- Saved quotes with dates and the trade's name
- A model number photographed off the plate of an appliance
- A text message where the painter confirmed the paint code
- A rough sketch of where the electrician decided to run a new cable

It doesn't have to be polished. In fact, it's better if it isn't — because the unpolished thing is the thing you'll actually do. A quick photo with your phone is a document. A one-line note in a notes app is a document. A screenshot of a message is a document.

What isn't a document is a conversation you're hoping you'll remember.

## The photos that matter most

If I had to pick the five types of photos that return the most value — not during the renovation, but months or years after — they would be:

### 1. Every room, before anything changes

Wide, well-lit shots of every room on day one. Not the tidy version after you've cleared the clutter — the actual version, because that's the record. A dated before shot establishes the baseline for insurance purposes, resale conversations, and the simple question: "what did this room look like before I knocked through?"

### 2. Every open wall, before it closes

This is the single highest-value photo type in a renovation, and the one people forget most often. Cables, pipes, junction boxes, the old chimney breast, the place the stud wall was — all of it disappears behind plasterboard. From that day forward, every question about it costs money to answer.

Photograph every open wall, floor, and ceiling the day before the plasterer arrives. Wide shots for context, close-ups at anything that carries water or current. Make sure the timestamp is accurate. You will not remember which week the bathroom wall closed. The photo's timestamp will.

### 3. The fixture with its model plate

Your dishwasher will last fifteen years. The manufacturer will not. When something breaks, the installer, the insurance company, or the parts supplier will want to know exactly what model you have. A quick photo of the data plate on the side or back of the appliance — the one nobody looks at until it's too late — saves an afternoon of detective work.

### 4. The before-and-after of finished trades

These are the photos that matter most when you resell. "We re-roofed it in 2024" is a claim. "We re-roofed it in 2024" with a dated photo of the old felt, the new underlay, and the finished slate is evidence. Future buyers, surveyors, and insurers respond to evidence.

### 5. The trade conversation you want remembered

A tradesperson will have a different memory of what was agreed six months after they've moved on to the next job. A photo of the wall with notes written on it with a dry-erase marker ("new socket here, 1200mm AFFL"), a text message confirming the plan, a photo of the sketch — anything that captures the decision in the moment is worth keeping.

## A system that doesn't require willpower

Documentation only works if it's part of the workflow, not an add-on. The friction has to be near zero. Here's a system that takes roughly five minutes a week:

### Photograph at the till

While the receipt is still in your hand, photograph it. Don't put it in a drawer. Don't wait until evening. The paper fades, and the eighth trip to the hardware store erases the seventh from memory.

### One place, every time

Pick one method and stick to it. Options include:

- A dedicated photo album on your phone (most phones can create albums called "Renovation — Kitchen" or "Renovation — Before")
- A folder on your computer, with dated subfolders
- An app like Home Stories that keeps photos, receipts, notes, and tasks all attached to the same project

The best system is the one you'll actually use when you're standing in a half-finished bathroom holding a wrench. A phone photo album is fine. What isn't fine is three different systems that all collect fragments and never reunite them.

### The weekly five-minute review

Every Sunday evening (or whatever day works for you), spend five minutes:

1. Open your photo album or folder
2. Sort new photos into their right project/room
3. Write one-line notes on anything unclear ("bathroom tile, selected 7/3" or "electrician quote — £3,200 for rewire")
4. Check that your backup is running

That's it. Five minutes. In a year that's 260 minutes.

## The payoff, when it arrives

Documentation doesn't feel useful during the renovation. It feels like homework. The payoff comes later, in moments you can't predict:

- **An insurance claim.** You're filing for water damage, and the adjuster asks what the original layout was. You open the app, find the before photos from week two, and the claim moves from "we'll investigate" to "approved."
- **Resale.** A prospective buyer asks what's behind the kitchen wall. You pull up the photo of the open wall from three years ago, and they see the full run of pipework. The conversation shifts from doubt to confidence.
- **Troubleshooting years later.** A plumber needs to drill near the bathroom wall. You send a photo showing exactly where the new cable was run during the renovation, and the job takes two hours instead of half a day of exploratory work.
- **Peace of mind.** The knowledge that the record exists, that it's organized, that you can open it on your phone in thirty seconds. That's worth the five minutes a week.

## A concrete example

A homeowner in Copenhagen refitted their kitchen in 2023. They photographed the old tiles before demolition, saved the invoice from the tiler, and noted the model of the new extractor fan on the back of the unit. Two years later, the extractor started vibrating — an unusual rattling that the new installer had never seen before.

Because they had the model number, the installer ordered the correct replacement part in forty-eight hours. If they'd called the original installer from 2023 and asked "do you remember what fan we bought?", they'd have been looking at weeks.

The photo of the model plate took three seconds to take. It saved three weeks.

## Start now, even if you haven't started renovating yet

If you're planning a renovation and haven't broken ground, start the photo system today. Photograph the house as it is now — every room, every angle. That becomes your day-one record, and the contrast with the finished work will be something you're glad to have.

If you're mid-renovation and haven't been documenting, start this week. It's not too late to photograph open walls, to snap the model plate of your chosen fixtures, to create the album that future-you will thank you for.

## The tools — or don't use tools

You can absolutely do this with a shoebox of receipts, the Photos app on your phone, and your memory. It won't be elegant, and fragments will get lost. But it will be better than nothing, and nothing is what most people end up with.

If you want the pieces in one place — photos, receipts, notes, tasks, budget — an app like [Home Stories](https://apps.apple.com/dk/app/home-stories-renovation-app/id6754754960) exists for that. It keeps everything attached to the project, exports it as a PDF when someone official asks, and works fully offline. But it's just a container. The habit — photographing at the till, saving the before shots, reviewing once a week — is what matters. The app is secondary.

## The bottom line

A renovation document is a small thing in the moment and a large thing in retrospect. It costs almost nothing to create — three seconds for a photo, thirty seconds for a note — and it returns its value in moments of stress: insurance claims, buyer questions, tradespeople drilling blind, the simple anxiety of "did I remember to…"

Take the photo. Save the receipt. Write the model number. Do it at the till, not later. Do it in one place, not scattered. Do it once a week, not never.

Future-you is already grateful. You just can't see them yet.
