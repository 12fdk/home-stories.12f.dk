There's a specific kind of regret that only shows up months after a renovation ends. It isn't about the work — the work is usually fine. It's about the *record* of the work: the thing you didn't photograph, the number you didn't write down, the conversation that lives only in two people's increasingly different memories.

None of these take skill to avoid. Every one of them takes less than a minute in the moment. Here are the eleven that come up again and again — from my own projects and from every renovator I've compared notes with.

## 1. Every wall, before it closed

The single most valuable photo in a renovation is the one of an open wall. Cables, pipes, junction boxes, the place the old chimney was — all of it disappears behind plasterboard, and from that day forward every question about it costs money to answer.

Photograph every open wall, floor, and ceiling *the day before* the plasterer or screed crew arrives. Wide shots for context, close-ups at anything that carries water or current. Make sure they're dated. You will not remember which week the bathroom wall closed; the photo's timestamp will.

## 2. Receipts, at the till

Not in a shoebox at the end of the month. At the till, while the paper is still in your hand — thermal receipts fade, and the eighth trip to the hardware store erases the seventh from memory. A photographed receipt with a date and a project attached is evidence: for the tax deduction when you sell, for the insurance claim, for the warranty argument. We wrote a whole [photo-first receipt system](/blog/how-to-organize-renovation-receipts/) about this, because it's the habit with the highest money-recovered-per-second ratio.

## 3. The quote next to the invoice

A quote is a promise; an invoice is what happened. Kept apart, they never get compared, and the gap between them — the extras, the "while we were in there", the hours that grew — is exactly where budgets die quietly.

Keep every quote, and when the invoice lands, put the two numbers side by side while you can still remember what changed. Sometimes the gap is fair. You want to *know*, either way.

## 4. Money you've committed but not paid

The kitchen is ordered. The electrician is booked. Nothing has left your account, and your spreadsheet says you're fine. You are not fine — that money is spoken for.

This is the single most common way renovators end up "suddenly" over budget: spent and committed were never tracked as separate numbers. The moment you sign anything, it belongs in the total. Watching **spent, committed, and remaining** as three numbers is the whole trick to [not going over](/blog/how-to-budget-a-home-renovation/).

## 5. The small stuff

Nobody's budget is killed by the kitchen. It's killed by screws, silicone, another brush, two more meters of cable, the parking, the skip that needed emptying twice. Individually invisible, collectively a four-figure line item that appears in no plan.

Log the small purchases with the same reflex as the big ones. If logging one takes more than ten seconds, your system is too heavy — that's a tooling problem, not a discipline problem.

## 6. Your hours

DIY isn't free; it costs evenings and weekends. Track the hours anyway — not to be grim about it, but because the number makes two decisions honest. First: whether the next job is really cheaper done yourself. Second: whether the timeline slipping is a mystery or just arithmetic.

## 7. Decisions, and the why

Paint code, tile batch, the model number of the mixer tap, which wall you decided *not* to move and why. Six months later, when a tile cracks or you're matching paint for touch-ups, the decision matters and the reasoning has evaporated.

One note per decision. What, when, why, and where it was bought. It reads like bureaucracy until the day it reads like genius.

## 8. The order of the trades

Electrician before plasterer. Floor before kitchen. Painter last, always, no matter what the painter says about being flexible. The sequence *is* the plan — and when a date moves, everything downstream moves with it.

Write the sequence down as tasks in phases, not as a feeling. It's the difference between rescheduling a chain and rediscovering it. The [phases guide](/blog/home-renovation-phases/) covers the standard order and what belongs in each.

## 9. What was actually said

"He said the extra socket was included." "She said week 14 was realistic." Verbal agreements run renovations — and memory is the worst possible storage medium for money-shaped promises.

After every meaningful conversation with a contractor, one line in the project notes: date, who, what was agreed. Not because anyone is dishonest — because everyone is human, and a dated note settles in ten seconds what an argument can't settle in an hour.

## 10. The contingency burn

A contingency isn't a lump; it's a fuel gauge. If you set aside 15% and you've burned 10% of it while the project is 30% done, that's not a footnote — that's the earliest, cheapest warning you will ever get.

Check the gauge at every phase boundary. Spent contingency never comes back; pretending otherwise is how "we were fine until suddenly we weren't" happens.

## 11. What "done" means

The last 5% of a renovation is a fog of almosts: the door that doesn't quite close, the missing skirting corner, the socket cover in a drawer somewhere. Without a written definition of done — a snag list, room by room — the fog becomes the permanent state of the house.

Write the list *before* the final walkthrough, walk it with the contractor, and date every item as it clears. The project ends when the list is empty, not when everyone is tired.
