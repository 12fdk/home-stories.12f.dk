You've just finished getting three quotes for your kitchen renovation. The lowest is $18,000. The highest is $29,000. That's a **56% spread** on what is, supposedly, the same job.

What's happening here? Who is overcharging and who is underestimating? How do you even begin to compare them?

The answer is that you can't compare quotes the way they're presented. Most contractors don't use the same structure, don't break costs down the same way, and often omit items entirely — either by accident or design. To compare them properly, you need a common framework.

This post walks through the **line-by-line method** for comparing contractor quotes, step by step. You'll learn how to strip each quote down to its component costs, spot what each one includes and excludes, and identify which quote delivers the best value — not just the lowest price.

## Why quotes are impossible to compare at first glance

A contractor quote is not a standardised document. Two contractors working on the same project might present their quotes in completely different formats:

- **One lists 25 line items.** Another lists 12.
- **One splits labour and materials.** Another lumps them together.
- **One includes waste removal.** Another assumes it's "included in labour."
- **One breaks out permits as a separate line.** Another adds a 5% "admin fee" to cover them.
- **One says "tile installation, mid-grade."** Another just says "tile."

None of these differences are necessarily dishonest. But they make it impossible to tell whether Quote A is genuinely cheaper than Quote B — or whether it's just cheaper because it's leaving something out.

The line-by-line method exists to solve exactly this problem.

## The line-by-line method: a step-by-step framework

The goal is to force all three quotes into the same structure so you can compare them directly. Here's how.

### Step 1: Print all quotes and highlight the differences

Start by printing every quote (or opening them side by side on screen) and highlighting anything that looks different: a line item one has and another doesn't, a different material specification, a different timeline. Don't annotate with judgments — just flag the differences.

### Step 2: Build a comparison table

Create a table with these standard columns and fill in each quote's data under each one. The categories below are the most common cost buckets in renovation work:

| Cost Category | Quote A | Quote B | Quote C |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Demolition & prep** | $1,200 | $1,000 | $1,500 |
| **Framing / structural** | $3,500 | $3,800 | $3,200 |
| **Plumbing** | $2,800 | $2,400 | $3,100 |
| **Electrical** | $1,900 | $2,100 | $1,800 |
| **Drywall** | $1,600 | $1,600 | $1,600 |
| **Flooring** | $2,200 | $1,800 | $2,600 |
| **Cabinetry** | $4,500 | $4,200 | $5,000 |
| **Countertops** | $2,100 | $2,100 | $2,500 |
| **Tile & backsplash** | $1,800 | $1,500 | $1,900 |
| **Paint** | $900 | $800 | $1,000 |
| **Fixtures & hardware** | $800 | $600 | $900 |
| **Waste removal / cleanup** | $600 | — | $700 |
| **Permits & inspections** | $400 | Included | $450 |
| **Contingency / allowance** | $1,000 | — | $1,500 |
| **TOTAL** | **$23,300** | **$21,900** | **$26,050** |

The total tells you nothing. The row-by-row breakdown tells you everything.

### Step 3: Normalize the line items

Where one quote has "Tile & backsplash: $1,500" and another has "Bathroom tile: $800 + Kitchen backsplash: $700," combine them. Where one has "Tile — $1,500" and another has "Tile: $1,200 + grout: $200 + thinset: $100," you need to ask the contractor what grade and type of tile they're using — that's where the $300 difference might live.

The goal is a **common taxonomy**: every quote expressed in the same categories, using the same names.

### Step 4: Flag the gaps

After normalizing, look for items that one or two quotes have but the others don't. These gaps are where the price differences hide:

- **Waste removal.** If one quote doesn't list it, is it hidden in the labour line? Or is it omitted entirely and will show up as a surprise later?
- **Permits.** Some contractors fold permit costs into overhead. Others list them explicitly. Know which one you're getting.
- **Cleanup.** End-of-project cleanup might be $400–$800 depending on the scope. A quote without it is either including it silently or leaving it out.
- **Contingency allowance.** A contractor who budgets for small unknowns ($500–$1,500) is being more honest than one who presents a clean, certain number.

### Step 5: Compare material specifications

The same trade name item can vary 3× in price depending on grade. If three quotes all say "cabinets," you need to know:

- What brand? (IKEA, Rockwell, custom cabinet maker?)
- What construction? (Plywood box vs. particleboard?)
- What finish? (Laminate vs. painted vs. veneer?)
- What hardware? (Standard hinges vs. soft-close?)

A quote that lists specific brands and grades is easier to compare and easier to audit after the work is done.

## Red flags: what the numbers are telling you

Once you have the normalized table, look for these patterns:

### 1. One quote is much lower than the others

A quote that's more than 15% below the next-lowest quote is worth scrutinizing. It doesn't mean the contractor is dishonest — but it does mean they've either:

- Omitted items (waste removal, permits, cleanup)
- Used lower-grade materials than the others
- Underestimated labour hours
- Planned to recover costs through change orders

**What to do:** Ask the contractor directly: "I notice your quote is $X lower than the others. What are you doing differently?" A transparent contractor will explain. A defensive one is the risk.

### 2. One quote is much higher than the others

Similarly, the most expensive quote might be including more (higher-grade materials, more detailed scope) or it might be padding. The line-by-line breakdown tells you which.

**What to do:** Ask for the same level of detail. "Can you break down the cabinetry cost by brand and grade?" If the detailed answer justifies the price, it's not padding.

### 3. Fewer line items than the others

A quote with 15 line items when the others have 30+ is a red flag. Either the scope is genuinely simpler (unlikely if all three contractors visited the same site), or items are being bundled into vague descriptions.

**What to do:** Ask for a more detailed breakdown. "Can you itemize the labour costs by trade?" If they can't or won't, walk away.

### 4. No exclusions list

The best quotes come with an "exclusions" section — a list of things the contractor *won't* do. Common exclusions: painting in areas not mentioned, moving furniture, working around existing occupants, landscaping restoration. A quote without exclusions is implicitly promising to handle everything — but you need to see that promise written down.

**What to do:** If there's no exclusions list, ask: "What's *not* included in this quote?" Write down the answer.

### 5. Large up-front payment

If one quote demands 50% or more before work starts, while the others request 10–25%, that's a warning sign. Standard practice is a deposit to lock in materials and schedule, with the balance drawn against milestones.

**What to do:** Compare payment schedules. A fair schedule ties payments to completed phases: 10% deposit, 25% after demo, 25% after rough-ins, 25% after installation, 15% at substantial completion, 10% at final walkthrough.

## A practical comparison example

Here's a real-world scenario from a kitchen renovation in a 1970s Danish home. Three contractors visited the same kitchen, reviewed the same plans, and returned with these quotes:

| | **Contractor A** | **Contractor B** | **Contractor C** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Total quoted** | **DKK 145,000** | **DKK 128,000** | **DKK 162,000** |
| Labour (all trades) | DKK 72,000 | DKK 60,000 | DKK 78,000 |
| Materials | DKK 48,000 | DKK 46,000 | DKK 55,000 |
| Waste removal | DKK 5,000 | Not listed | DKK 7,000 |
| Permits | DKK 6,000 | DKK 6,000 | DKK 6,000 |
| Payment terms | 25-25-25-15-10 | 40-40-20 | 20-30-30-20 |
| Timeline | 8 weeks | 6 weeks | 10 weeks |
| Exclusions | 5 listed | None | 8 listed |

**What's going on:**

- **Contractor B** is cheapest but has the most red flags: no waste removal line, no exclusions, and a 40/40/20 payment schedule (80% before completion). The $17,000 savings relative to A is partly real but partly missing — waste removal alone is ~DKK 5,000, and the aggressive payment terms increase your leverage risk significantly.

- **Contractor A** sits in the middle on price, has a balanced payment schedule, lists exclusions (which tells you they've thought about what could go wrong), and provides waste removal. The trade-off: slightly shorter timeline and slightly lower-grade materials.

- **Contractor C** is the most expensive but offers the longest timeline (more realistic), highest-grade materials, and the most comprehensive exclusions list. The DKK 17,000 premium over B is explained by material quality and labour hours — the line items back it up.

**The verdict:** Contractor A is the best value for most homeowners. Contractor C is worth choosing if budget allows and you prioritise quality over speed. Contractor B is the risk: the gap is real, but it exists because of omissions and payment risk, not because they're a better deal.

This is exactly the kind of comparison that's invisible if you just look at totals. The line-by-line method makes it visible.

## Summary: your comparison checklist

Before you choose a contractor, run through this checklist:

1. ☐ **Three quotes minimum** for the same scope
2. ☐ **Normalize the categories** — all quotes expressed in the same cost buckets
3. ☐ **Compare line by line** — not just the totals
4. ☐ **Flag gaps and omissions** — what does one quote have that another doesn't?
5. ☐ **Check material specifications** — same trade name, same grade?
6. ☐ **Read the exclusions** — what's each contractor *not* doing?
7. ☐ **Compare payment schedules** — avoid anything that demands 50%+ upfront
8. ☐ **Ask directly about the gap** — why is one quote 30% different from the others?
9. ☐ **Trust the pattern, not the outlier** — if two quotes cluster and one is far away, the outlier needs explanation
10. ☐ **Choose value, not cheapest** — the best quote is the one whose scope and price align with your priorities
